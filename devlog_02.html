Dev Log · Issue 002
The Joy of Drawing Outside the Lines
The Sealed Door: Idle Chronicles · Design Journal
April 2026 Godot 4 Design → Balance Solo Dev

Things have been going well. Genuinely, quietly well. There's something almost meditative about sitting down at the end of the day and just creating. No meetings. No tickets. No one to tell me the feature is out of scope. When making a game was just a half-formed daydream, I used to catch myself thinking "wouldn't it be cool if...?" and then letting it go. Now I get to find out.

Being the sole owner of a project is strange and wonderful. Every decision is mine. Every terrible idea is also mine, which is a humbling check on the whole thing. But the freedom to chase a thought all the way to its conclusion, or its dead end, is something I didn't expect to love as much as I do.

An Idea, Iterated

Let me walk you through a design loop that I think captures what this phase has felt like. Early on, I knew I wanted a strong equipment system. My love for the Diablo series runs deep. That itch of watching loot drop and wondering is this the one? is something I want players to feel here too. So I started writing out all the different loot tiers and rarity classes.

Then a thought arrived, as thoughts do: wouldn't it be cool if you could level up your equipment, like in Disgaea? And of course it would be cool. So I went deeper. Every single piece of equipment would have its own level track. You'd invest in it, watch it grow.

Final Design
Essence Investment
Player-driven leveling for Rare gear and above only

But as I was writing it, a feeling crept in. Who actually wants to level a Common piece of equipment? You find it at level 5, maybe it carries you for a day, and then a Rare drops and it's obsolete. Putting real investment into something designed to be replaced felt like a slog, both for the player and for me trying to make it meaningful. Back to the drawing board.

"Throwing something out is the design process working exactly as intended."

The answer that stuck was to tie equipment leveling to the rarity gates. Common and Uncommon gear is disposable by design. It drops, does its job, and gets salvaged. Rare, Epic, and Legendary pieces are worth investing in. You spend Essence (a universal resource earned by salvaging the things you don't keep) to push them from +0 all the way to +10, each step increasing their stats and adding a visible glow to their name.

Will that turn out to be the right call? Genuinely no idea. That's what the balance phase is for. But it feels right, and at this stage, feeling right is enough to keep moving.

Almost Out of the Design Phase

Speaking of balance, I'm close to starting the balance phase. The bones of the game are solid. Class system, combat, equipment, the town and its buildings, quest and discovery layers, the economy. All of it is documented. What remains now is flesh: numbers, tuning, the spreadsheets that will tell me whether any of this actually works at scale.

I'll be honest: I am a little bit of a spreadsheet nerd. There is genuine joy for me in building a stat scaling table and watching a curve behave itself. Or misbehave. Both are informative.

Core Design · Classes, combat, equipment, economy, systems
GDD Foundation · Save architecture, project structure
Balance Phase · Stat spreadsheets, tuning curves, economy friction
Build Phase · Combat loop, enemy system, full UI

I've been reading up on idle game balancing specifically. There's a lot of conventional wisdom out there: exponential curves, softcap patterns, the usual frameworks. I'll probably follow most of it, up to the point where following it stops being fun. My guiding principle for this phase is simple: tune it to make it fun. If that means breaking the rules, so be it. I'd rather ship something that feels good and doesn't match the textbook than something theoretically correct that nobody wants to play.
